The Rules of Composting

1 : To Learn Compost, One Must Compost

Having a good understanding of the natural process that turns raw organic
materials into rich soil is essential for any gardener or farmer. One method of achieving
this is through aerated composting, which involves introducing airflow into the compost pile.
In addition to airflow, heat is also necessary to destroy pathogens, weed seeds, and parasites,
all of which can be harmful to plants. Pathogens are disease-causing germs that should not be
introduced into the soil, while parasites, such as worms, can be beneficial for soil health. However,
not all worms are equal, and gardeners may prefer specific species, such as Eisenia foetida and Lumbricus
rubellus, over others. Ultimately, creating rich soil is a complex and ongoing
process that requires a combination of knowledge, skill, and patience.







2 : Oxygen Is The Secret To Composting

When it comes to composting, oxygen is a vital component in the process.
Aerobic composting involves the decomposition of organic matter using microorganisms that
requires oxygen to break down the materials. This process not only creates nutrient-rich
soil but also produces heat that can destroy pathogens, parasites, and weed seeds.
To ensure the oxygen level in the compost pile remains at 10% or higher, it's important
to turn the compost regularly. Interestingly, the air we breathe at sea level contains
20% oxygen, making it abundantly clear how crucial oxygen is to the composting process.
To start the composting process, fill the bin with your mix and take a temperature reading
to establish your starting point. Once you turn the blower on, you should see a change in
temperature from 300 to 500 degrees Fahrenheit within 12 to 24 hours.
Following these simple steps will help you create nutrient-rich soil for your plants and garden.







3 : Water Is Life

Water is often referred to as the "magic ingredient" in composting, and
for good reason. Aerobic microorganisms, which are essential to the
composting process, require a film of water to live and thrive. This film
of water allows populations of fungi and bacteria to grow rapidly throughout
the compost pile, breaking down organic matter into nutrient-rich soil.
Additionally, water plays a critical role in reducing the odor of the compost
pile. However, it's important to get the water content just right.
If the compost pile is too wet, with a water content over 65%, the pore
spaces will open up, and the excess water will result in terrible odors.
On the other hand, if the compost pile is too dry, with a water content
below 50%, the microbiology of the system will crash, and it will not work.
While a dry pile will eventually happen, it can be renewed by adding water, but not
too much. Achieving the perfect water content in the compost pile is crucial for
creating healthy, nutrient-rich soil.







Composting has two stages: the active phase and the curing phase. The active phase lasts for 21 to
30 days and is led by bacteria that break down sugars, proteins, and carbohydrates, generating
heat. The curing phase is dominated by fungi that break down the remaining organic matter. It can
take several months, and during this phase, mushrooms may grow, and white filamentous material may be
visible in the pile. The curing phase is crucial to create stable and usable compost for gardening.
